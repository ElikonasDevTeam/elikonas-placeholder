Land of the Free Because of the Brave

This weekend I took a road trip with a friend — down from Bremerton, through Portland, and along stretches of the Pacific Northwest where the land still carries the memory of the Oregon Trail. Not in any dramatic way. No wagon ruts, no markers every mile. Just long, open roads cutting through terrain that once asked everything of the people who crossed it: people who left behind everything familiar in search of a life that didn't exist yet. People who bet on something they couldn't see.

This is America's 250th birthday year. And out there on those roads, with the miles rolling by and the afternoon light doing what it does in the Pacific Northwest, I kept thinking about what it means that we can take a trip like this at all — that we can wander down a highway for the joy of it, follow our curiosity, and come home safely. That kind of freedom doesn't maintain itself.

Land of the free because of the brave.

I haven't lost anyone close to me in combat. I want to be honest about that. I don't have that particular grief to carry to the memorial. But I have people in my life who wore the uniform, and they've shaped how I understand what service actually looks like — not as an abstraction, but as a real choice made by real people with real lives.

My son served six years in the Air Force. Six years of his life given to something larger than himself, in service of a country that doesn't always make it easy to love. I watched him grow into that commitment. I watched it change him. Whatever he carried during those years — and I didn't always know what that was — he came home, and I am grateful every day for that fact.

My cousin went a different direction. He served in the Navy, and when his time was done, he went on to fly commercial airlines. That alone would be a remarkable second act. But then he went further — he became one of the pilots who flies aircraft directly into hurricanes for NOAA research. Into the storm. On purpose. So that scientists on the ground could understand what was happening, and so that communities in the path of those storms could have a little more time. I think about that choice sometimes. Most people spend their lives trying to get out of the way of dangerous things. He spent his career flying toward them because someone had to, and he knew how.

The Paths People Take

I knew someone once — I'll leave the specifics out — who was one of the most intellectually capable people I've ever encountered. He spoke multiple languages, most of them self-taught. He could pick up a new subject and master it faster than people who had spent years studying it formally. His mind was genuinely extraordinary.

But he couldn't do school. Traditional classroom learning, with its structure and its timelines and its requirement that you sit still and follow the prescribed path — none of it worked for him. He'd drift. Get bored. The system kept telling him, in one way or another, that he didn't fit. He went through jobs the same way — brilliant at every one of them until the novelty wore off, and then unable to make himself stay.

And then he joined the Army. Specifically, Army intelligence — a field that put his unusual mind to use and gave him something school never had: structure with stakes. A framework with genuine purpose. For the first time, the discipline wasn't arbitrary. It was in service of something real.

He thrived.

I've thought about him a lot while building Elikonas. Because his story is one that schools failed to tell properly: here was a person of exceptional capability who simply needed a different container. The traditional system looked at him and saw someone who couldn't follow instructions. The military looked at him and saw an asset. The difference wasn't his intelligence — that was constant. The difference was the context.

What We Build for the People Who Stay

On Memorial Day, we honor the fallen — the ones who made the final sacrifice, who never came back to the lives they left behind. That loss is specific and irreplaceable, and I don't want to move past it too quickly. Those lives mattered. Every single one.

But I also find myself thinking about what we owe the living. The veterans who come home and don't know what comes next. The people who found their footing in military service and are now standing in a civilian world that doesn't quite speak their language. The ones — like the person I knew — who needed a particular kind of structure and purpose to become who they were meant to be, and who are now trying to figure out what that looks like outside of a uniform.

And the ones who never served but who share something with them: a learning style that doesn't fit the standard mold. A kind of intelligence that doesn't show up on transcripts. A need for purpose before they can commit to the process.

That's who Elikonas is for.

We exist, in part, because of everyone who protected the freedom to learn and pursue a different kind of life. The whole premise of this platform — that every person deserves access to education in a form that actually fits them, that knowledge doesn't belong only to those who can navigate a traditional system, that a learner-owned record of real skills and real growth has value regardless of where it was earned — none of that is possible in a country where that freedom isn't protected.

So on this Memorial Day, I want to say thank you. To the fallen, who gave everything. To the living who served and carry that service with them. To my son, my cousin, and everyone else who raised their hand and said: I'll go.

The road trip was beautiful. The roads were clear. We got home safe.

None of that is guaranteed. None of it is free. Someone always pays for it.

We should remember that.
